On April 8, 2026, a devastating explosion struck, causing mass casualties and widespread displacement. UPEL immediately activated its child protection emergency response protocol, deploying staff across two governorates to identify, document, and protect affected children. Within the first 72 hours, the team managed 29 child protection cases spanning family tracing, casualty documentation, and psychosocial support. Later that afternoon, a second explosion hit. Our social workers — many displaced themselves — continued working through both blasts.

Protection files & child justice.

Beyond IDP response, UPEL continued its core mandate — protection case management, court representation, and judicial follow-up across all six governorates. Even during the war. Even while our social workers were displaced. March 2 – April 16, 2026.

During the same period that UPEL was responding to the IDP crisis, 122 children in conflict with the law (offenders) and 66 children in contact with the law (victims of crimes) required judicial follow-up, social investigation, and court attendance across six governorates. These are not IDP cases — these are children in the regular child justice system whose cases could not wait.

Custody and displacement complicated everything: phone calls were made to check if children in existing protection files had changed addresses. Social workers tracked down families who had been displaced mid-case. Court hearings continued — 85 court sessions attended in March and April alone, the vast majority in Mount Lebanon.

In parallel, 15 new protection files were opened and 60 protection follow-ups conducted. The system never stopped.

"Our caseworkers were themselves displaced — sleeping in shelters, on colleagues' floors — and still showing up at the courthouse the next morning to represent a child."— UPEL Coordination · April 2026

What we learned.
What we ask.

▲ Critical

The dual burden is unsustainable.

UPEL is running two full operations simultaneously — IDP emergency response and regular child justice case management. 95 IDP children plus 188 children in contact with law plus 85 court hearings, with displaced staff. This cannot hold without additional resourcing.

● Strength

Field activation was immediate.

From March 2 through April 16, no region went dark. On April 8, teams were documenting cases within hours of both explosions. Court attendance continued the next day. Social workers who were displaced themselves returned to work.

→ Ask to partners

MOPH coordination must continue.

When hospitals were overwhelmed on April 8, MOPH facilitated registration for our cases. This coordination pathway — between child protection actors and health facilities — must be formalized and sustained.

◆ Gap

PSS and legal capacity are under-dimensioned.

7 PSS sessions and 7 legal support interventions for IDP children, against 16 sexual violence and 16 domestic violence victim cases in the regular system. The psychologist and legal hours are stretched impossibly thin.
